4-7-8 Breathing for Sleep: Does It Actually Work?

By Tom | February 2026 | 5 min read

the 4-7-8 breathing technique was popularised by Dr Andrew Weil. he called it a "natural tranquilliser for the nervous system." bold claim. but loads of people swear by it for falling asleep.

I was sceptical. then I tried it during a week where I couldn't sleep because of work stress. it didn't knock me out like a sleeping pill, but it genuinely helped. here's how it works and what to actually expect.

The technique

it's simple. three phases, one ratio: 4-7-8.

Inhale
4
Hold
7
Exhale
8
  1. Inhale through your nose for 4 counts.
  2. Hold your breath for 7 counts.
  3. Exhale slowly through your mouth for 8 counts. Make a gentle whooshing sound as you exhale.

that's one cycle. do 4 cycles to start. as you get more comfortable, you can extend to 8 cycles.

the counts don't need to be seconds. the ratio is what matters. if 4 literal seconds feels too long for the inhale, count faster. the point is that the exhale is always twice as long as the inhale, with a long hold in between.

Why it works for sleep

the mechanism is the extended exhale. when you exhale for longer than you inhale, you activate your parasympathetic nervous system. this is the "rest and digest" branch of your autonomic nervous system, the opposite of fight-or-flight.

the 7-count breath hold is the interesting part. holding your breath increases CO2 levels slightly, which has a mild sedative effect. it also forces your body to slow down oxygen consumption, which is calming.

the combination of long exhale + breath hold makes 4-7-8 more sedating than most other breathing patterns. box breathing (4-4-4-4) is calming but alert. 4-7-8 is calming and sleepy. the ratio difference is why.

Dr Weil recommends doing 4-7-8 twice daily for best results: once in the morning (just to practise the technique, not for sleep) and once at night. he says the effect gets stronger with regular practice over 4-8 weeks.

What to expect the first time

your first few attempts will probably feel awkward. the 7-count hold can feel uncomfortably long. you might feel lightheaded. that's normal and usually goes away after a few cycles as your body adjusts.

most people don't fall asleep during their first session. it takes practice. what you will notice is that your mind quiets down. the counting gives your brain something to do instead of replaying your to-do list or that awkward thing you said in a meeting three years ago.

after a week of nightly practice, most people report faster sleep onset. not instant sleep, but "I'm actually drowsy" instead of "I've been lying here for 45 minutes."

4-7-8 vs box breathing for sleep

both are good for calming down. but they have different effects:

  • 4-7-8 is better for sleep because the extended exhale and long hold actively promote drowsiness. it's designed to put you down.
  • box breathing (4-4-4-4) is better for calm focus. it settles your nerves without making you sleepy. good for before a presentation, not great for 11pm when you need to actually sleep.

if you're lying in bed and want to sleep: use 4-7-8. if you're stressed during the day and need to calm down without getting drowsy: use box breathing.

Tips for making it work

  • lie on your back. tongue on the ridge behind your upper front teeth. this is the position Dr Weil recommends. it keeps your airway open and makes the exhale whoosh sound natural.
  • dim the lights first. the technique works better when your environment already signals "sleep" to your brain. phone away, room dark, then start breathing.
  • don't force it. if 7 counts feels too long for the hold, shorten all the counts proportionally. 3-5-6 works fine. the 1:1.75:2 ratio is what matters.
  • be patient. this isn't a sleeping pill. it's training your nervous system. most people need 1-2 weeks of nightly practice before the effect is reliable.
  • use it with a timer. counting manually keeps your brain engaged in a way that undermines the relaxation. an app that guides the timing lets you actually let go.

When it might not work

4-7-8 breathing helps with the "can't switch off" type of insomnia. the kind where your body is tired but your mind won't shut up. it gives your mind a simple task and triggers a parasympathetic response.

it's less effective if your insomnia is caused by something physical (pain, sleep apnoea, medication side effects) or a clinical sleep disorder. breathing exercises are a tool, not a cure-all. if you've been struggling with sleep for weeks, talk to your GP.
