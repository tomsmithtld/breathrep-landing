Wim Hof Breathing Method: A Beginner's Guide

By Tom | February 2026 | 6 min read

wim hof breathing is one of those things that sounds too weird to work. you sit down, hyperventilate on purpose, hold your breath, and somehow feel amazing afterwards. I was sceptical the first time I tried it. then I held my breath for 2 minutes on my first attempt and my scepticism vanished.

here's exactly how to do it, what's happening in your body, and what to watch out for.

The basic technique

Wim Hof Breathing (3 Rounds)

1
30 power breaths. Breathe in deeply through the nose or mouth (belly first, then chest). Let go of the exhale. Don't force the out-breath. Just let it fall. Repeat 30 times.
2
Exhale and hold. After the 30th breath, exhale fully and hold. Don't breathe in. Start your timer. Hold as long as you comfortably can. Beginners: 1-2 minutes is normal.
3
Recovery breath. When you feel the urge to breathe, inhale deeply. Hold that inhale for 15 seconds. Then let go.
4
Repeat. That's one round. Do 3 rounds total. Each round, your retention time usually gets longer.

the whole thing takes about 15 minutes. 20 if you're relaxed about it.

What's happening in your body

the 30 power breaths are controlled hyperventilation. you're blowing off CO2 (carbon dioxide), which makes your blood more alkaline. this has a few effects:

  • your breath-hold reflex gets suppressed. the urge to breathe is driven by CO2 levels, not oxygen levels. with less CO2 in your blood, you can hold your breath much longer than normal. that's why beginners can suddenly hold for 2 minutes.
  • adrenaline spikes. a 2014 study at Radboud University showed that Wim Hof method practitioners had significantly higher epinephrine (adrenaline) levels. this produces that buzzy, alert feeling during and after the session.
  • the tingling. you'll feel tingling in your hands, feet, and sometimes face. that's the CO2 drop causing blood vessel constriction and temporary pH changes. it's normal and harmless.

What to expect the first time

honestly, it's intense. not painful, but intense. here's a rough first-timer experience:

  • breaths 1-15: "this is just breathing, what's the big deal"
  • breaths 15-25: tingling starts, lightheadedness, you might feel slightly dizzy
  • breaths 25-30: proper buzzing, possible light-headedness, feeling of warmth
  • hold phase: surprisingly calm. the urge to breathe takes longer to arrive than you'd expect
  • recovery breath: a deep satisfying inhale. your whole body relaxes
  • after round 3: euphoria is a strong word, but it's not far off. clarity, calm energy, heightened awareness

the feeling after 3 rounds is genuinely remarkable. it's not meditation-calm and it's not caffeine-alert. it's somewhere between. your mind feels quiet but your body feels switched on. it lasts about 30-60 minutes.

Common mistakes

  • forcing the exhale. the in-breath should be active and full. the out-breath should be passive. just let your chest deflate. don't push the air out.
  • breathing too fast. this isn't speed breathing. each breath should take about 2 seconds. 30 breaths at a steady pace takes about a minute.
  • holding too long. your ego will want to beat your previous time. don't push it. the sweet spot is "comfortable challenge", not gasping and panicking.
  • doing it standing up. sit or lie down. the lightheadedness is real. people have fainted doing this standing up.

When and how often

morning is best. the adrenaline response means it wakes you up. doing it before bed can make it harder to sleep (though some people find the opposite).

frequency: daily is fine if you want to. 3-4 times a week is plenty to feel the benefits. Wim Hof himself recommends daily practice, but even once a week is better than never.

pair it with cold exposure for the full protocol. do the breathing first, then cold shower. the breathing makes the initial cold shock much more manageable.

safety warning: never do Wim Hof breathing in or near water. the breath hold can cause shallow water blackout (sudden loss of consciousness). people have drowned doing this in pools, baths, and open water. always practice on dry land, seated or lying down. if you have epilepsy, a heart condition, or are pregnant, talk to your doctor first.
